## The Big Picture: Swedish Youth Unemployment

**Headline figure (SCB/AKU, 15–24):** ~24% in 2024 — but over half of these are full-time students who also happen to be job-searching. The internationally comparable NEET rate (not in employment, education, or training) is only **7%**, well below the EU average of 11%.

**Which number to use:**
- The 24% is what politicians argue about. It is real but inflated by the student count method.
- The 7% NEET rate is the harder, more meaningful number for genuine exclusion.
- **Both are trending worse since 2022.**

**EU comparison:** Sweden ranks 3rd highest in the EU on the headline rate, behind only Romania and Spain. This is partly a statistical artefact — Germany, Denmark and the Netherlands all have apprenticeship systems that classify trainees as *employed*. But the structural problem is genuine. Sweden lacks equivalent transition infrastructure.

**Who is most affected:**
- Foreign-born youth and those without completed upper secondary diploma
- **Recent graduates** — new and politically charged: 1 in 4 newly graduated academics earns below student-loan level four months after finishing (Saco 2025). Numbers up 35% since 2022.
- Young women: more affected than young men since the 2020 pandemic, reversing a historical pattern.

## Our Research: Canaries in Sweden

*Lodefalk et al. (2025/2026), using full-population employer-employee register data (AGI) + complete Platsbanken job ads (4.6 million, 2020–2026)*

### Key finding

Within the same employers, young workers in AI-exposed occupations are losing ground — while older workers gain.

| Age group | Relative employment change (2025H1) |
|-----------|-------------------------------------|
| 22–25 years | **−5.5%** vs. low-AI occupations |
| 26–30 years | −4.9% |
| 50+ years | +1.3% |

**This effect is accelerating.** From modest in 2023 to −5.5% by early 2025. It is not a one-time shock — it follows an adoption curve.

### The posting puzzle — and why it misleads

Job postings in Sweden fell ~30% since 2022. This looks like AI destruction. **It is not.**

The decline began in **April 2022** — seven months before ChatGPT launched — exactly when the Riksbank raised rates for the first time. Controlling for monetary tightening, AI exposure adds zero additional aggregate decline. The Riksbank, not AI, drove the posting drop.

But this aggregate calm hides a **recomposition within firms**: employers post similar numbers but hire fewer junior workers per position.

### The mechanism

AI does not eliminate jobs — it changes *who gets them*. Entry-level tasks (coding, data aggregation, drafting, analysis) are the ones AI handles best. Employers can manage with one experienced worker and an AI tool where they previously needed two, including a junior.

Swedish employment protection law (LAS, "sist in — först ut") concentrates adjustment on the most recently hired, amplifying the effect on young people.

### Gender dimension

Young women face **twice the adjustment** of young men in the same cohorts (−1.6% vs. −0.7% average). Partly occupational sorting; partly AI exposure in feminised white-collar roles.

### Comparison

A parallel US study (Brynjolfsson, Chandar & Chen, Stanford, 2025) finds a 16% relative employment decline for 22–25-year-olds in AI-exposed US occupations. Our Swedish estimate (−5.5%) is smaller — consistent with Sweden's later and slower AI adoption curve. **The effect is not hypothetical. It is measurable, in both countries, right now.**

## Q1: Hur påverkar generativ AI ungas chans att få sitt första jobb?

**Core answer:** It makes it harder, through a specific mechanism: AI automates the entry-level tasks that junior hires would otherwise perform, so employers need fewer of them. It is not a wave of layoffs — it is a hiring slowdown that is nearly invisible in aggregate statistics.

**Data point:** In our Swedish register data, young workers (22–25) in AI-exposed occupations are 5.5% less likely to be employed within a given firm by early 2025, compared to those in less-exposed occupations — and the gap is growing.

**Nuance:** This is not universal. In occupations where AI *augments* rather than *substitutes* — where the AI is a co-pilot rather than a replacement — demand for skilled workers can increase. The problem is concentrated in the entry-level cognitive tasks that young workers spend their first years learning.

---

## Q2: Vilka jobb är det främst som försvinner eller förändras?

**Most exposed entry-level roles:**
- Junior software developers / coders (debugging, testing, routine code)
- Finance and audit trainees (model-building, data aggregation, report drafting)
- Administrative and back-office roles (data entry, billing, scheduling)
- Paralegals and document reviewers
- Content writers, copy editors, translators

**Common thread:** Structured, language-based, cognitive work — exactly what junior hires spend their early career doing, and exactly what large language models do best.

**What is *not* disappearing:** Roles requiring physical presence, tacit craft knowledge, interpersonal judgment, or contextual professional discretion. Construction, care, trades. Also: senior roles where AI assists rather than replaces, and new roles managing and overseeing AI systems.

**Skills rising in value:** AI literacy (using and critically evaluating AI outputs), judgment under uncertainty, relational and communication skills, domain expertise, creative direction (specifying what to make, not just making it).

**The paradox for young people:** To develop judgment, you need experience. AI is removing the entry-level work through which that experience has historically been built. That is the structural challenge — and it is not yet solved.

---

## Q3: Varför är det här en viktig att följa?

**Three reasons:**

1. **It is already happening, and accelerating.** Our data show a −5.5% effect in 2025H1 vs. essentially zero in 2023. This is not a prediction — it is a measurement. The canary is already in the mine.

2. **It is invisible in standard statistics.** Aggregate unemployment did not spike in Sweden. Job postings fell because of monetary tightening, not AI. Surface indicators look calm. But within firms, who gets hired is changing. Without employer-level data at monthly frequency, you miss it entirely. That is the value of our data.

3. **The long-run stakes are high.** Young people who cannot enter the labour market miss the accumulation of human capital and social networks that compound over a career. A generation with a rocky start does not just catch up automatically. We know this from earlier recessions. With AI, the pattern may be stickier because the technology keeps improving — the bar for entry rises continuously.

**What should be done:**
- Monitor employer-level hiring composition, not just aggregate unemployment.
- Invest in AI skills in upper secondary and university programmes — practical AI literacy, not just awareness.
- Examine whether apprenticeship or internship models can substitute for the entry-level work AI is displacing.
- Watch the social insurance gap: graduates who cannot find work and have no a-kassa coverage are invisible to policy but fully real.
